The Fleuss does not pretend to be an icon, and that is exactly why I trust it. It borrows sixties dive-watch design, runs an automatic movement, and takes its name from the man who invented the closed-circuit rebreather, all at a price the vintage originals and their Swiss descendants left behind decades ago. It is built for people who want the feel of the era without the auction.

<p class="pullquote">Both objects answer the same question honestly.</p>

That is the whole reason it belongs with the Grenadier. When Land Rover retired the old Defender and replaced it with something more comfortable, a different company stood up and decided to build the honest version itself. That is the microbrand move carried out at the scale of a car. Both objects exist because the original maker either discontinued a design or priced it out of reach, and someone stubborn decided the design still deserved to exist.

I will answer the obvious objection before it arrives. The Grenadier costs real money and the Fleuss does not, so this pairing is about philosophy rather than price. That is fine, because the match was never about the money. It is about two makers who refused to let a good idea die simply because its inventor lost interest.

The brown leather strap it shipped on did not fit my wrist, so it has lived on a green NATO since the first week. The green suits the Lagoon dial better than the strap the marketing team chose, which is the first argument in miniature. The honest version is the one you adjust to fit your actual life.
